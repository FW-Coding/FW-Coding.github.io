Projects

A selection of technical work spanning battery modelling, parameter estimation, electrochemical diagnostics, and energy system design. These projects combine physics-based simulation with experimental data to address practical challenges in battery technology.

Transmission Line Models for Lithium Plating

Developing a novel transmission line (TL) architecture to model and detect lithium plating on graphite anodes. This work combines electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) data, custom distribution of relaxation times (DRT) inversion, and atomic force microscopy (AFM) measurements to quantify plating severity and validate model predictions. The approach offers a new diagnostic pathway for identifying early-stage degradation and informing safe charging protocols.

Conducted in collaboration with the Bazant group at MIT and Imperial's Electrochemical Science and Engineering (ESE) group as part of my master's thesis.

Parameter Estimation Pipeline

Built a complete parameter estimation workflow that processes raw cycling data from Novonix cyclers, extracts key features, and fits Doyle-Fuller-Newman (DFN) model parameters using customised particle swarm optimisation (PSO). The pipeline achieves approximately 50 mV error in voltage predictions across multiple operating conditions, demonstrating accurate capture of cell behaviour.

As part of this work, I contributed column mapping for the Novonix cycler to the open-source PyProBE package, enabling smoother integration of experimental data into battery modelling workflows.

Electric Race Car Battery Packs

Led a team of five students designing and building bespoke battery packs and battery management systems (BMS) for Imperial Racing Green's electric race car. Responsibilities included modelling track energy requirements to determine optimal capacity and discharge rates, performing cell selection, executing pack assembly (spot-welding, soldering), and integrating the system with the vehicle's powertrain.

The team coordinated extensive testing and validation to ensure safe, high-performance operation, ultimately achieving strong competition results and demonstrating the practical application of battery engineering principles.

Finite-Volume PyBaMM Implementation

Implemented the first example of PyBaMM applied to a complex fluid-dynamics style system using a finite-volume method in Python. This work extended PyBaMM's capabilities beyond standard electrochemical models and demonstrated the versatility of the framework for multiphysics problems.

For effectively communicating this technical work to diverse audiences, I won a Student Science Communicator award and received the best poster prize at a PyBaMM/FUSE event. The project was completed during a summer internship with the Faraday Institution's FUSE programme.
