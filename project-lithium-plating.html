Transmission Line Models for Lithium Plating

Master's Thesis | MIT & Imperial College London | 2024–2025

Developing a novel transmission line (TL) architecture to model and detect lithium plating on graphite anodes. This work combines electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) data, custom distribution of relaxation times (DRT) inversion, and atomic force microscopy (AFM) measurements to quantify plating severity and validate model predictions.

Overview

Lithium plating is a critical degradation mechanism in lithium-ion batteries that occurs during fast charging or low-temperature operation. It reduces capacity, increases internal resistance, and poses safety risks. Traditional detection methods struggle to identify early-stage plating before significant damage occurs.

This project develops a transmission line model that captures the spatial distribution of plating along the electrode. By fitting the model to EIS data across multiple frequencies, we can infer plating location, severity, and progression over time.

Key Techniques

  • Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS): Non-destructive characterisation across a wide frequency range to capture electrode dynamics
  • Distribution of Relaxation Times (DRT): Custom inversion methods to deconvolve overlapping impedance features
  • Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM): Direct validation of plating morphology and distribution
  • Transmission Line Architecture: Spatially-resolved model capturing plating heterogeneity

Impact

This approach offers a new diagnostic pathway for identifying early-stage lithium plating and informing safe charging protocols. Unlike post-mortem techniques, EIS-based detection can be performed in-situ, enabling real-time monitoring and adaptive charging strategies.

Collaboration

Conducted in collaboration with Professor Martin Bazant's group at MIT and the Electrochemical Science and Engineering (ESE) group at Imperial College London as part of my master's thesis research.
