Design Studio: Drill Teardown & Redesign

Materials Science & Engineering | Imperial College London | 2023–2024

A comprehensive design and engineering project focused on the teardown, characterisation, and redesign of a hand-held drill. This multi-phase project combined reverse engineering, materials analysis, mechanical design, and technical documentation to understand existing products and propose improvements.

Project Overview

The project involved systematically disassembling a commercial hand-held drill, characterising its components and materials, analysing the design decisions, and proposing an improved redesign. This hands-on approach provided insight into manufacturing processes, material selection, cost optimization, and design for assembly.

Project Phases & Reports

The project was documented through three comprehensive reports tracking progress from initial analysis through to final redesign:

Preliminary Report

Initial teardown and component inventory. Background research on drill mechanisms, motor types, gear systems, and material requirements. Preliminary analysis of existing design with identification of strengths and potential improvement areas.

Grade: 75%

View Report

Interim Report

Detailed characterisation of materials and components using microscopy, mechanical testing, and analytical techniques. Analysis of manufacturing processes and cost structure. Development of preliminary redesign concepts with justification based on materials science principles.

Grade: 80%

View Report

Final Report

Complete redesign proposal including material selection, manufacturing process specification, cost analysis, and performance predictions. Detailed technical drawings and specifications. Comprehensive comparison with original design demonstrating improvements in performance, manufacturability, or cost.

Grade: 80%

View Report

Key Technical Skills

  • Reverse Engineering: Systematic disassembly and documentation of complex mechanical systems
  • Materials Characterisation: Identification and analysis of component materials and their properties
  • Mechanical Design: Understanding of gear systems, motors, bearings, and power transmission
  • Manufacturing Analysis: Assessment of production methods including injection molding, die casting, and assembly
  • Cost Engineering: Material and manufacturing cost estimation and optimization
  • Technical Communication: Professional report writing with clear justification of design decisions

Design Considerations

The redesign process required balancing multiple competing factors including performance, cost, manufacturability, ergonomics, durability, and environmental impact. Material selection decisions considered mechanical properties, processing requirements, cost, and sustainability. The project demonstrated the complexity of engineering design and the importance of systematic analysis.

Skills Developed

This project strengthened practical skills in reverse engineering, materials analysis, CAD modeling, manufacturing process understanding, and technical documentation. The structured approach developed ability to analyze existing products critically and propose evidence-based improvements grounded in materials science and engineering principles.
