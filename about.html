About

My journey into battery science started at Imperial College London, where I quickly gravitated toward the intersection of materials science, electrochemistry, and computational modelling. From my first-year introduction to energy materials, I've pursued almost every opportunity to work on batteries—from building race car battery packs to developing sophisticated parameter estimation pipelines.

Currently completing my master's thesis on transmission line models for lithium plating detection, I'm spending this year on exchange at MIT, deepening my understanding of electrochemical systems and advanced diagnostic techniques. I've been fortunate to work with leading research groups, contribute to open-source battery modelling tools, and present my work at national conferences.

Beyond research, I've led technical teams, mentored peers as a course representative, and developed strong skills in technical communication—whether explaining complex models to researchers or presenting battery pack designs to engineering judges.

What drives me is the challenge of bridging simulation and experiment: building models that are not only mathematically rigorous but also validated against real-world data. Battery science sits at a unique intersection of chemistry, physics, and engineering, and I'm excited to contribute to the next generation of energy storage systems.
